Verifiable Receipt Standard

VRS — A High-Level Overview

What it is, how it works, why it matters, and how you might build on it.

VRS makes an ordinary receipt verifiable, private, and useful after the purchase. It does not put your shopping basket on a public ledger, and it does not pretend a blockchain can settle who legally owns a thing.

The pitch

VRS makes a receipt verifiable and useful after the purchase. It does not make the receipt public, and it does not pretend a blockchain can settle legal title.

It is an open standard. There is no protocol fee, no token, and no rent on the core. Neutrality is the point: a standard that charged rent would not be adopted, and would not deserve to be.

The problem

Today a receipt is a PDF, an email, or a row in a merchant's database. That makes it:

  • hard to verify — anyone can fake a PDF, and there's no portable way to check who issued it;
  • easy to lose — when the email is gone, the proof of purchase is gone;
  • poor for resale — a buyer cannot confirm the item was genuinely bought, or by whom;
  • clumsy for warranty — the claim depends on finding the original paper;
  • useless to software — a smart contract or accounting system cannot read or trust it.

Receipts are one of the most common documents in the economy, yet they carry almost none of the trust they could.

The obvious fix, "put receipts on a blockchain," is worse than the problem. It would publish everyone's purchase history, charge gas on billions of transactions a day, and still not say who owns anything. VRS takes a different route.

The value

What it's worth

A digital receipt is already worth a lot. The GS1 / Auto-ID Labs digital-receipt white paper lists the gains: searchable purchase history, smoother returns, warranty in one place, expense and budget tracking, loyalty, safety recalls, and an end to paper and thermal (BPA) waste. All of it rides on structured product data (GTINs) rather than scanned paper.

VRS gives you all of that, and more, because its receipt is not merely digital. It is verifiable, transferable, private, and stateful on a shared chain. That changes a receipt from a convenient record into a portable credential. It works for people who do not trust the merchant; it survives the merchant going out of business; and it follows the item to its next owner.

What only a verifiable, transferable receipt can do

Portable warranties. The warranty travels inside the receipt, so it transfers with the item. A second-hand buyer inherits whatever cover is left; the claim can be proved without revealing the price; and none of it depends on anyone keeping the paper. The warranty becomes a property of the item rather than of the person who happened to file the receipt.

Theft protection. Register a valuable once. If it is stolen, flag it on a shared status registry, and any buyer, marketplace, or pawnbroker can check whether it is stolen before handing over money. Theft pays mainly because stolen goods resell easily; make the check cheap and that market shrinks. No single retailer could run this registry, because it has to be trusted by parties who do not trust each other, which is what a neutral registry is for. (The standard case is a tradesperson whose van is emptied of a few thousand pounds of tools overnight.)

Recalls that reach the current owner. A recall is a signed notice anchored against the manufacturing batch. Each owner's wallet checks it, locally, against the items the owner holds; there is no contact database and no tracking. This reaches the second and third owner, whom a retailer's mailing list never had. Those are the people a safety recall most needs to find.

Owner-gated communities, feedback, and rewards. A product's forum, its rewards, and, most useful of all, its reviews can be restricted to people who hold the item now. A verified-owner review cannot be botted or bought, because the reviewer proves current ownership (or first purchase) and nothing more. The same gate runs owner-only releases, firmware, and loyalty, and these pass to the next owner on resale rather than staying with the first buyer.

Tamper-proof service and inspection history. Each service, repair, or inspection is a signed record whose hash is anchored in time. The log is therefore tamper-evident, and, more important, it cannot be backdated: a seller cannot fabricate years of service the week before a sale. A complete, time-proved history is expensive to fake, so it is a useful signal; it raises the value of an item that has been looked after, and it travels with the item.

Counterfeit detection, within honest limits. Manufacturer-endorsed serials let VRS notice when a serial is cloned — used twice — and show that an item came through an authorised channel. That defeats the casual counterfeit and the grey market. It does not, on its own, prove the physical object is genuine: that still needs inspection, a secure tag, or a Digital Product Passport. VRS supplies the digital half of the evidence and is careful to say so.

In short: a digital receipt is a better record; VRS makes proof of purchase a portable, verifiable, programmable credential that outlives the merchant and follows the item.

A note on privacy and power

Privacy by design, or surveillance by default

Pointed the wrong way, a standard that makes receipts and ownership verifiable is a surveillance instrument: a complete, queryable record of what everyone owns and buys. Surveillance capitalism already sells purchase data, and authoritarian states already use ownership and purchase records to control people. Built carelessly, this would hand both a sharper tool. The risk is real, and we treat it as a constraint on the design, not a footnote.

The honest reality, though, is that verifiable ownership and purchase records are coming anyway, by law. Digital identity wallets (eIDAS 2.0 and the EUDI wallet), age verification, e-invoicing mandates, anti-counterfeit serialisation, and the EU Digital Product Passport are all turning the once-paper record of what you bought and own into something a machine can check. The question is no longer whether this happens, but who builds it and on whose terms.

So there is a plain case for building it openly, now, and getting in ahead of the surveillance-by-default version. It is far better that the standard for verifiable ownership be one where the consumer holds the data, discloses it selectively, puts nothing on a public ledger, keeps no central registry of who owns what, and charges no fee and issues no token — than to let the defaults be set by a system built for tracking first. The privacy is not decoration. It is the reason to prefer this to the alternatives that are coming either way.

VRS does not claim to solve surveillance. It claims something smaller and reachable: to make the verifiable-ownership infrastructure that is arriving regardless default to the user's control rather than someone else's. A version that was not private by construction would be worse than the paper it replaces, and we would rather it were not built than built that way.

The core idea

A receipt is a signed off-chain document. The chain is a shared status layer, not the receipt database.

What you bought, what you paid, and who you are stay in a signed document that lives in your email, a file, or a wallet. The chain is touched only when several parties who do not trust each other must agree on a fact about state: who holds an item's rights now, whether it was returned or reported stolen, or when a service record provably existed.

The split buys three things at once: privacy (purchase data never lands on a public ledger), scale (there is no per-receipt gas, and most receipts never touch the chain), and legal honesty (the chain records control of an entitlement, not legal title).

Part 1

Core ideas and components

Two objects, not one

The most important decision in VRS is to split the receipt into two objects:

Fiscal / basket receiptItem entitlement
What it isEvidence of who paid what, whenA right or claim attached to one line item
LifecycleImmutable; kept for tax/expense/auditActive → returned / voided / stolen / transferred
Transferable?No — it's the payer's tax recordYes — follows the item through gift or resale
LivesOff-chain, in email/file/walletOff-chain document + optional opaque on-chain state

The reason is simple. The payer's fiscal receipt should never become a public transferable token: that would leak private spending and corrupt tax records. The item is different. The warranty, return right, and resale evidence attached to it do need to move from one owner to the next. Keeping the two apart is what lets the chain stay opaque while the item stays useful.

A receipt proves what was bought. An entitlement follows the item. That distinction is the heart of VRS.

Commercial core + fiscal evidence

Countries do not agree on what a "receipt" is. An EU VAT invoice, a Japanese qualified invoice, an India GST e-invoice, a China digital fapiao, and a US expense receipt are related but not the same object. Forcing them all into one global schema is a dead end. VRS keeps the common part small and binds the rest by profile:

  • The commercial core proves the commercial event — issuer, time, line items, quantities, disclosed amounts, and the commitments needed to derive entitlements. It verifies anywhere, with no knowledge of any country's tax regime.
  • A fiscal evidence profile binds the local tax artefact (its identifier, document hash, QR payload, status) without dragging its rules into the core.

A verifier can then say exactly what it checked, and no more:

✓ VRS signature valid ✓ commercial core valid ✓ fiscal evidence bound ✓ India GST profile verified ⚠ tax compliance not assessed

The components

No single party is trusted for the whole system. VRS is a small set of cooperating roles:

ComponentTrusted forNot trusted for
IssuerThe statements it signsPhysical truth, legal title
Claim / ref serviceCustody and availabilityMaking an invalid receipt valid
Holder agentKey handling, transaction intentLegal ownership, physical condition
RegistryShared state and orderingOff-chain document contents
VerifierApplying its own policy—

The claim/ref service is the adoption wedge: it makes receipts useful before anyone has a wallet. The holder agent is the user's control surface once they want stateful workflows like resale or warranty.

Part 2

Cryptography and signatures

VRS is built from a few well-understood primitives, put together so that a holder can prove exactly the one fact a verifier needs, and nothing else.

Canonical encoding

Everything that gets hashed must serialise the same way on every machine, or signatures won't match.

  • Hash function: keccak256 throughout.
  • Canonical JSON: JCS (RFC 8785), with a compact CBOR/COSE profile for NFC tags and fiscal devices.
  • On-chain-reconstructable values: Solidity abi.encode, so a contract can recompute the same bytes.
  • Timestamps are Unix seconds; binary is lowercase 0x hex; implementations reject malformed input rather than silently normalising it.

The commitment tree

A receipt is structured as a Merkle tree. The header is leaf 0, each line item is a leaf, and the merchant signs the root.

Each line-item leaf is itself a small commitment over groups: core, commercial, warranty, descriptive, provenance. Absent groups use a fixed "absent" sentinel. This sub-leaf split is the key to privacy: a verifier can be shown core (what was bought) without commercial (what was paid).

Selective disclosure — no ZK required

To prove a line item, a holder reveals the sub-leaves the situation calls for, plus the Merkle path, plus the signed root. The verifier rehashes upward to the root and checks the signature.

Disclosure levelReveals
Full documentThe entire receipt
Core onlyHeader + selected line core (no price)
Selected groupsHeader + whatever groups the verifier requires
Zero-knowledge (future)Proves a statement about the receipt, layered on the same commitments

These are VRS disclosure levels — how much a proof reveals — written full / core / core+<group> / zk. The term has nothing to do with Ethereum layer-1 / layer-2.

This matters most when a contract consumes a proof on-chain: a disclosed leaf is then public forever, so the price must not ride along by default. Plain Merkle disclosure covers most needs today. Zero-knowledge proofs are an upgrade, not a prerequisite (see Forward compatibility).

Entitlement IDs, salts, and bearer secrets

Each line item carries a random 32-byte entitlement salt. The on-chain entitlement ID is derived from it:

saltCommit    = keccak256(abi.encode(T_SC,  entitlementSalt))
entitlementId = keccak256(abi.encode(T_ENT, receiptRoot, lineIndex, saltCommit))

The crucial property: documents and proofs only ever carry saltCommit, never the raw salt. So:

  • the chain registry keys on entitlementId and learns nothing about the product, price, or merchant;
  • whoever holds the document and the salt can claim the entitlement (a bearer instrument) — which mirrors how most physical receipts already work;
  • someone shown a proof (which carries only saltCommit) cannot register or claim the entitlement.

Bearer secrets live in a separate payload from the receipt document, and travel separately. This is why VRS has two link types:

vrs1:ref:<url>     — powerless, safe to hand a desk or employer
vrs1:claim:<url>   — carries bearer authority; only for the holder, redeemed once

Desks get vrs1:ref. Holders get vrs1:claim. Never the other way around.

Signatures and issuer identity

VRS is signature-scheme-agnostic, with sensible defaults:

  • EIP-712 typed-data signing where the issuer key is an Ethereum key.
  • COSE / X.509 where the issuer is already a fiscalisation device.
  • EIP-712 + ERC-1271 for the holder challenge (so smart-account wallets work too).

A signature only proves a key signed. It becomes "this issuer signed" only under the verifier's policy. VRS does not require a global identity system or a DID. A verifier may instead accept any of: a local keyring, a small signed issuer manifest at /.well-known/vrs/issuer.json, an anchored manifest hash, or an attestation from a marketplace, PSP, GS1, or regulator it already trusts.

Issuers run a boring key hierarchy so a leaked key is not a catastrophe:

The root never signs routine receipts. Live keys are short-lived, scoped, and individually revocable — if one is compromised, the issuer revokes it, publishes the affected window, and rotates a new one without destroying the issuer identity.

Asserted vs proven time

A signature proves who and what — never when. A key can sign any date. So VRS distinguishes:

  • Asserted time — the issuedAt field inside the signed document. Sufficient for ordinary sale receipts.
  • Proven time — the document hash is included in an anchored Merkle root published to the chain, proving it existed by a given block.

Anchoring is optional for an ordinary sale receipt, but it carries the weight for provenance. The main attack on a resale history is not forgery; it is backdating: inventing years of service records the week before a sale. Proven time defeats this, because a record anchored in 2026 demonstrably did not exist in 2024, whatever date it carries. The reference verifier marks any history entry that has only asserted time.

What VRS proves — and what it doesn't

VRS provesVRS does not prove by itself
A recognised issuer signed the receiptTax / accounting compliance
The root commits to the disclosed fieldsLegal title or ownership
A line item exists in the receiptPhysical possession
An entitlement derives from receipt + line + saltPhysical genuineness
The registered holder controls it (via challenge)Complete lifecycle history
A lifecycle hash existed by an anchored timeThat the issuer's statements are honest
A serial matches a manufacturer batch commitment—

The honesty is deliberate. VRS speaks of holder, registered holder, and entitlement control, and never of unqualified "ownership." Physical authenticity still needs inspection, a secure tag, or a legal warranty; VRS supplies the digital half of the evidence, and labels it as such.

Privacy roadmap for resale

Transfer is the one step that writes to a public chain, so it is where privacy needs the most care. Today the registry stores holder and firstHolder as plaintext addresses and emits Transferred(from, to) events, so a passive observer can reconstruct an item's whole ownership chain. (The addresses are opaque only until one of them is linked to a person.) The plan is layered, and each step is useful on its own:

  • Stealth-address holders (ERC-5564/6538). Each holder is a fresh address only they can spend from, unlinkable to their identity — provided gas is paid externally (a relayer or ERC-4337 paymaster), otherwise the funding address re-links the holder.
  • Holder commitments + a nullifier set in place of the plaintext holderOf mapping (the Zcash/RAILGUN note model, adopted natively so VRS keeps its own status semantics and composability). Transfer becomes a note re-commitment, not a public owner update.
  • ZK proofs for the proving paths — a replay-protected membership proof to satisfy a smart contract, and selective "first / Nth holder" disclosure when a buyer wants it — over the same commitments.

Net residual leak: a chain observer sees only that some entitlement had an event (a sale or update happened), never who holds it or its ownership history. See Forward compatibility.

6 · Lifecycle history — the chain stores a hash, not the document

A repairer signs a service attestation; only its hash is logged on-chain. The full document stays private and can be selectively disclosed at resale.

Walk through the data — documents, signatures & chain state

7 · Manufacturer authenticity — detect clones, without overclaiming

Each serial is committed against a manufacturer batch root, and a nullifier marks a serial as used, so a cloned serial is detectable.

Walk through the data — documents, signatures & chain state

VRS can say: "Serial authenticated; channel verified; this serial not previously seen." It cannot say "this physical object is genuine" — that still needs inspection or secure tags.

Provenance for resale is a three-part check: a Digital Product Passport proves what the item is; the entitlement proves who holds the rights; the receipt proves where and when it was sold. None alone is provenance; together they are.

8 · Recall — reaches the current owner, names no one

A recall keyed to the manufacturing batch reaches whoever holds the item now, second- and third-hand owners included, and reaches them without a contact database. The manufacturer broadcasts once; each wallet matches it against the batches it holds.

Walk through the data — documents, signatures & chain state

VRS supplies the authenticated notice and the broadcast-by-batch primitive — not a transport, a relay, or a contact list. The anchored notice is constructive notice to every holder of the batch; opt-in push (wallet messaging, relays) adds reach to passive owners by the owner's own choice.

9 · On-chain consumption — a smart contract is the verifier

In every case so far the verifier was a human or a server. The capability that is hard to get any other way is this: a smart contract checks a proof of purchase itself and acts on it — a loyalty reward, a token gate, an escrow release — with no merchant API, no off-chain voucher, and no one's permission. Proof of purchase becomes a composable credential.

Walk through the data — documents, signatures & chain state

The verifier is stateless and permissionless: one signature recovery, a handful of hashes up the Merkle path, one attester lookup — cheap on an L2. The consuming contract layers its own policy on top. Three properties make this safe and private:

  • Bind to the item, not the presenter. Rewards and one-time perks key on the entitlementId, so an item can't farm a reward again each time it's resold — consumed once, forever.
  • Core-only disclosure. The chain learns what was bought (inherent to a product-specific reward) but never what was paid — the price sub-leaf stays off-chain.
  • Issuer policy is the contract's. The verifier checks the signature against an accepted attester set; the contract can additionally gate on, say, an authorised retailer — no merchant cooperation required at claim time.

The same mechanism turns a delivery receipt into deterministic escrow: sale, delivery, and service are one signed format with three issuer roles, so an escrow contract releases funds on a courier-signed delivery proof exactly as a reward contract pays out on a sale proof — one spec, three oracles.

The state layer

Why a chain at all?

Use the chain only where no single party should be the database:

  • returns across branches and retailers;
  • entitlement transfer and current holder;
  • stolen / voided / returned flags;
  • lifecycle event hashes (proven time);
  • serial nullifiers;
  • consumption by smart contracts (rewards, escrow, gating).

Everything private stays off-chain. The chain provides shared state and proven ordering — nothing more. The document layer is chain-neutral; the default state profile is Ethereum-aligned because the most interesting downstream uses are smart contracts, ERC-1271 accounts, and composable rewards.

Step 6 — Sign the root (EIP-712)

The merchant signs a typed-data digest over (receiptRoot, schemaHash). The domain is chain-agnostic (no chainId), so the same receipt verifies anywhere:

schemaHash    = 0xe18b2d7e…6c456993     # keccak256("vrs/receipt/1.0")
signingDigest = 0xd0709378…30ab9dc0
issuerSig     = ECDSA(signingDigest, issuerKey)

Step 7 — Entitlement ID and membership proof

The toaster's on-chain entitlement ID — opaque, leaking nothing about product or price:

entitlementId = keccak256(abi.encode(T_ENT, root, 1, sc1))
              = 0x47cc2d0c…b8cb979d

To prove the toaster line is in the signed receipt, a verifier is given leaf item1 (index 2), the path [PAD, n01], and folds it using the bits of the leaf index (2 = 0b10):

start = item1
bit 0 = 0 (left):  node(item1, PAD) = n23   ✓
bit 1 = 1 (right): node(n01,  n23) = root  ✓ matches receiptRoot

The fold reproduces root exactly — confirming membership while disclosing only item1's opaque siblings and the internal nodes.

The contracts

Key contracts and standards

VRS's on-chain layer is a small set of contracts — the reference implementation of the canonical Ethereum State Profile. The document layer stays chain-neutral; these are only what a verifier or holder reads and what a consuming contract builds on. Addresses come from the signed deployment manifest; a noncanonical deployment MUST NOT be treated as VRS state. The reference contracts are draft and unaudited, and live in Reference/04-reference-contracts/src.

Identity registry — AttestationRegistry (+ AnchorRegistry)

Turns "a key signed" into "an attested merchant / manufacturer / courier." It is verifier-relative — each consumer picks which attesters it accepts — and is not a DID system: roles are bytes32 tags (keccak256("merchant"), …) and it pairs with the off-chain issuer manifest. AnchorRegistry is the companion time layer: anchoring a batch root upgrades asserted issuedAt to proven time.

Registry — EntitlementRegistry

The shared ownership / status / lifecycle state, keyed by the opaque entitlementId. Registration is consumer-pull (the issuer never transacts) and offers a commit–reveal variant for public mempools.

struct Entitlement { Status status; address holder; address firstHolder; address issuer; }
// Status: Unregistered | Active | Returned | Voided | Stolen

function register(IssuerBinding b, uint256 lineIndex, bytes32 salt)   // + registerCommitted (commit–reveal)
function transfer(bytes32 id, address to)
function transferWithAuth(bytes32 id, address to, uint64 deadline, bytes holderSig)
function recordEvent(bytes32 id, bytes32 attestationHash)             // logs a hash, never contents
function flagStolen(bytes32 id) / clearStolen / markReturned / void
function holderOf(bytes32 id) returns (address)

Privacy: holder and firstHolder are plaintext today, and Transferred(from, to) events expose the ownership chain. The privacy-preserving variant below is the planned fix — see the resale privacy roadmap.

Manufacturer nullifier — MintAllowanceRegistry

Serial authenticity and clone detection, plus mint-allowance / channel assurance. Revealing a serial salt sets a set-once nullifier; a second use of the same serial reverts — that is the clone signal. Deliberately transparent (the serial/batch on-chain is fine), unlike the holder scheme which must hide.

mapping(bytes32 => bool)    nullifierUsed;     // set once; second use reverts (clone)
mapping(bytes32 => bytes32) boundEntitlement;  // nullifier => entId
// nullifier = keccak256(T_NULL, batchId, serial, …);  serial ∈ serialRoot (T_SNODE tree)

function authenticate(...)            // reveal serialSalt, set nullifier, bind to entId
function authenticateCommitted(...)   // commit–reveal (front-running defence)

Proves serial / receipt / channel evidence — never physical genuineness. The same set-once-nullifier + commit–reveal machinery is what the private holder registry reuses (domain-separated).

Privacy-preserving registry PLANNED

The forward variant of EntitlementRegistry: replace the plaintext holder mapping with a commitment accumulator (an incremental Merkle tree of holder "notes") plus a published nullifier set. Transfer becomes a spend-and-recreate to a stealth address; proving ownership becomes a ZK membership proof (replay-scoped by a per-context nullifier) rather than a public holderOf read.

Reuses the MintAllowanceRegistry nullifier pattern and commit–reveal, with its own domain tag and an added ZK/hiding layer. Full design: Reference/03-extensions-and-analysis/private_ownership_transfer.md.

Stateless verifier — ReceiptVerifier

The bridge between an off-chain proof and an on-chain consumer. It verifies a line-item proof (issuer signature + Merkle path + delegation) against its attester policy and returns the facts a contract needs. Permissionless and stateless; consuming contracts layer their own policy on top. It enforces the delegate's role (a key delegated for merchant can't act as manufacturer).

constructor(IAttestationRegistry attestations, IAnchorRegistry anchors, bytes32 role, address[] attesters)

function verifyLineItem(HeaderDisclosure h, CoreDisclosure c, …)

Example — an ownership-gated contract (RewardDistributor + variants)

The canonical consumption pattern is one call: verifyLineItem → (entId, productIdHash, identity) → apply program policy. The shipped example rewards verified purchasers; the same shape gates anything on ownership.

function claim(HeaderDisclosure h, CoreDisclosure c) {
    (, bytes32 entId, bytes32 productIdHash, address identity) = VERIFIER.verifyLineItem(h, c, …);
    require(authorisedRetailer[identity]);          // optional grey-market gate
    require(!claimed[entId]);  claimed[entId] = true;   // ★ ONCE PER ITEM — bind to entId, never the presenter
    points[msg.sender] += rateFor[productIdHash];   // reward; chain learns WHAT, never the price
}

Three flavours of the same verify → gate pattern:

GateHow
Reward purchasers (first registrants)Bind once-per-item to entId (the ★ above). For "original buyer only," gate on firstHolder — which never changes through resale.
DAO voting for current ownersVoting power = items where holderOf(entId) == voter and status == Active. Rights move with the item on resale; the manufacturer doesn't curate the voter set.
IoT config for the current ownerA contract maps entId → owner-controlled config (e.g. a callback address). The device reads the current authorised address; on transfer the new holder updates it — so the sensor reports to whoever currently holds the item, with no manufacturer in the loop.

Under the privacy-preserving registry, "current owner" gating doesn't need a plaintext holderOf: the owner submits a ZK proof "I hold an Active entitlement for product X" with a per-context nullifier — so a DAO vote, reward claim, or device update verifies ownership without revealing which item or who the holder is.

Related standards

Where it fits

VRS is not trying to replace the standards that already touch receipts and products. For the most part it binds to them, sits beneath them, or fills a gap they leave. In one line: VRS is the verifiable, private, merchant-rooted receipt-and-ownership layer, and it is designed to align with — not reinvent — product identity, fiscal e-invoicing, verifiable credentials, and the EU Digital Product Passport.

What VRS should align with, not reinvent

  • Product identity — GS1 GTIN and Digital Link (it already does).
  • Passport join — GS1 Digital Link and EPCIS events as the bridge to the manufacturer's DPP.
  • Credential presentation — a W3C VC 2.0 / SD-JWT VC bridge, so a VRS proof can be shown in an EUDI-style wallet. (SD-JWT VC is still a draft, so this is a target to track, not yet a fixed dependency.)
  • Fiscal — keep binding Peppol / EN 16931 / national artifacts by profile, rather than re-specifying them.

The upshot: VRS competes only with the scattered attempts at a signed e-receipt, and beats them on verifiability, transfer, and privacy. Everywhere else it is complementary. The one thing it genuinely owns — the layer no existing standard provides — is the transferable, private entitlement: ownership that can move between people and be proved without a central database. That is also the least-standardised space, which is the reason to build VRS rather than try to assemble it from the others.

Forward compatibility

Designed to leave room

A standard you cannot extend is one you will replace. VRS freezes only the surface it must, the v1 commitment format, and pushes everything else (new fiscal regimes, signature schemes, zero-knowledge predicates) out to profiles and version boundaries. The installed base of signed receipts keeps verifying while new capability is added on top.

What already keeps the door open

  • Agility at version boundaries, not inside every receipt. One commitment scheme per major version, and every domain-separation tag is suffixed :v1 — so a future suite gets its own namespace with no collision. The signature commits a schemaHash, giving each receipt an explicit version anchor.
  • Disclosure is structural. The five sub-leaf groups let a future predicate target one group (say commercial) without re-opening the rest. Selective disclosure was built in, not bolted on.
  • The bearer salt is already a proof witness. The entitlement-ID derivation was shaped so the salt doubles as a nullifier — the basis for unlinkable, prove-once predicates later, at the cost of roughly one extra in-circuit hash.
  • Signature-scheme agility. Issuer identity lives in a manifest that declares its keys, and holder checks run through ERC-1271 smart accounts — so a new signature suite (including post-quantum) can arrive per-issuer without a format break. The keccak commitments are already quantum-reasonable; the signature is the only real exposure.

Keccak versus zero-knowledge

VRS commits everything with keccak256 — ideal for on-chain reconstruction, but the most expensive hash to prove inside a zero-knowledge circuit. Proving a Merkle path or opening a group hash in ZK is cheap only on STARK-class or lookup-based provers. This is the single choice that shapes a ZK future, so it is stated plainly rather than buried:

Decide whether ZK predicates must work over existing keccak-v1 receipts (→ commit to a STARK-class prover, or recursion) or only over a future commitment suite (→ a SNARK-friendly hash the issuer also commits to). Both are viable; only the second is a format change, which is why the version anchor matters.

Levers kept open

Predicate proofs — "price > X", "GTIN in this set", "spent over €1000 this year" — are a genuine expansion of what's expressible, and they're where today's encoding choices bite. None is needed now; each stays cheap to add later because of a decision made now:

The cheap insurance worth nailing down

Almost everything above degrades gracefully to "introduce it in a v2 suite" — provided every receipt is unambiguous about which commitment suite it is under, and verifiers negotiate it. That single property is essentially free to guarantee in v1 and unblocks all the rest, so it is the one forward-compatibility rule worth making airtight before the format freezes.

Adoption

How you'd adopt it

Do not try to launch the whole network at once. Start where a single merchant and a single customer get value immediately, and add the stateful features later.

Pilot 1 needs no wallet, no registration, no chain — just signed receipts, refs, credit notes, and expense forwarding. Lead with the verifier (accounting platforms, warranty desks, insurers, marketplaces) — they already suffer from unverifiable receipts.

A first implementation is small:

  • Issuer / ref service: sign .receipt, generate bearer secrets separately, expose vrs1:ref and one-time vrs1:claim, serve a credit graph, support .vrsbox export — and never leak secrets through refs.
  • Holder agent: import .receipt / vrs1:claim / .vrsbox, preserve the document/secret split, register valuables only when useful, sign holder challenges, and show holder-vs-legal-title caveats.
  • Verifier: verify signature and root, check selective proof paths, reject bearer secrets in desk flows, read canonical registry state where needed, and verify credit documents independently.
