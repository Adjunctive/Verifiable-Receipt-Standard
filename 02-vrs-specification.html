Draft Community Group Specification

Verifiable Receipt Standard (VRS)

Version 1.0 — signed off-chain receipts, on-chain status, and composable proof of purchase

Abstract

This specification defines the Verifiable Receipt Standard (VRS): a standard for cryptographically verifiable digital receipts and receipt-derived item entitlements. A VRS receipt is a signed, off-chain document committing to a Merkle tree of a header and line items; a shared, chain-resident status registry records entitlement ownership, status, lifecycle event hashes, and serial authentication. VRS supports selective disclosure of receipt fields, transferable item-level entitlements, credit (return/correction) graphs, manufacturer serial authentication, batch-keyed recall notices, and consumption of proofs by smart contracts. It deliberately separates what a signature proves (an issuer made a statement) from legal title, physical possession, and physical genuineness, which it does not establish.

This document is the normative specification, with sufficient detail for an independent implementer to produce conforming issuers, holders, verifiers, services, and registry contracts. The companion VRS Overview is the primary informative source; the Reference/ tree (formal specification draft, reference Solidity contracts, conformance vectors, and design notes) is a secondary source. Two companion documents complete the implementer's kit: the VRS Data Formats (canonical JSON for every document type) and the VRS Conformance Vectors (byte-exact test fixtures).

Status of This Document

This is a draft. It is unaudited and not yet suitable for production deployment with real value. The wire and commitment format described here is frozen as VRS major version 1 (see §4 and §6); the prose continues to evolve toward a stable 1.0. Implementations claiming conformance must reproduce the normative test vectors of Appendix A byte-for-byte.

Where this document and the reference Solidity library VRSLib disagree on a hash construction, VRSLib and the conformance vectors are authoritative; report the discrepancy.

1. Introduction Informative

A receipt today is a PDF, an email, or a row in a merchant's database: hard to verify, easy to lose, useless to software, and poor for resale, warranty, and returns. Putting receipts directly on a public blockchain would publish everyone's purchase history and cost gas on billions of transactions. VRS takes a different route, captured in one sentence:

Core idea A receipt is a signed off-chain document. The chain is a shared status layer, not the receipt database.

Everything private — the products, amounts, and parties — stays in a signed document held by the user. The chain is written only when parties who do not trust each other must agree on a fact about state: who currently holds an item's rights, whether it was returned or reported stolen, when a service record provably existed, or whether a manufacturer's serial has been seen before.

VRS distinguishes two objects. The fiscal (basket) receipt is non-transferable evidence of who paid what, kept for tax, expense, and accounting. The item entitlement is a right derived from a single line item that may be registered, transferred, used for warranty or resale, and connected to lifecycle history. A commercial core verifies anywhere; jurisdiction-specific tax evidence is bound by profile rather than forced into one global schema. The remainder of this document specifies these objects and the operations on them. For a narrative treatment, see the Overview.

2.3 What VRS proves and does not prove

VRS proves: that a recognised issuer signed a receipt commitment; that a receipt root commits to disclosed fields; that a line item exists in a receipt; that an entitlement ID derives from a receipt root, line index, and bearer salt; that a registered holder controls an entitlement (given a holder challenge); that a lifecycle event hash existed by an anchored time; that a credit document references an origin receipt; that fiscal evidence is bound by hash or reference; and that a serial authenticates against a manufacturer batch commitment.

VRS does not, by itself, prove: tax or accounting compliance; legal title; physical possession; physical genuineness; complete lifecycle history; the honesty of an issuer's statements; or universal legal enforceability of a warranty or return right. A conforming user interface must not represent a VRS signature as any of these. It should use terms such as holder, registered holder, and entitlement control rather than unqualified ownership.

4. Global encoding rules Normative

  1. The hash function is keccak256 (the Keccak-256 used by Ethereum, not NIST SHA3-256).
  2. On-chain-reconstructable values must be encoded with Solidity abi.encode (32-byte left/right padded ABI encoding), as specified field-by-field in §6.
  3. Canonical JSON must use the JSON Canonicalization Scheme, [RFC 8785].
  4. JSON integers whose values are used for hashing or protocol state must be decimal strings unless otherwise specified.
  5. Binary values in JSON must be lowercase 0x hex strings.
  6. Implementations must reject invalid lexical forms rather than silently normalising them.
  7. Timestamps must be Unix seconds.
  8. Receipt documents and ordinary proof objects must not contain bearer secrets.

5. Schema identifiers Normative

Every VRS document and wire object must carry a schema string identifying its type and major version. A verifier must reject an object whose schema is unknown to its policy. The schema string is for routing and policy; it is informational and must not be treated as part of any keccak commitment. Cryptographic versioning is carried by the receipt schemaHash and by the frozen v1 commitment tags (§6.1).

6. Cryptographic constructions Normative

This section is the heart of the wire format. All constructions are frozen for VRS major version 1. A conforming implementation must reproduce every value in Appendix A exactly.

6.1 Domain-separation tags

Each tag is keccak256 of an ASCII label. Every hash is domain-separated by a leading tag so that, for example, a core hash can never collide with a header leaf or an internal node.

6.2 Group, leaf, and node hashes

Each line-item leaf commits to five groups; an absent optional group uses the ABSENT sentinel in place of its group hash. The constructions are:

coreHash        = keccak256(abi.encode(T_CORE, uint256 lineIndex, bytes productId,
                                        uint256 qtyMilli, bytes32 saltCommit))
commercialHash  = keccak256(abi.encode(T_COMM, uint256 unitPriceMinor, uint32 taxRateBps,
                                        bytes3 currency))
warrantyHash    = keccak256(abi.encode(T_WARR, bytes warrantor, uint32 durationDays,
                                        bytes32 termsHash))
descriptiveHash = keccak256(abi.encode(T_DESC, bytes32 jcsHash))   // jcsHash = keccak256(JCS(descriptive))
provenanceHash  = keccak256(abi.encode(T_PROV, bytes32 batchId, bytes32 serialSaltCommit))

itemLeaf   = keccak256(abi.encode(T_ITEM, coreHash, commercialHash, warrantyHash,
                                  descriptiveHash, provenanceHash))
headerLeaf = keccak256(abi.encode(T_HEADER, address issuer, uint64 issuedAt, uint8 receiptType,
                                  bytes32 fiscalHash, bytes32 delegationHash))
node(l,r)  = keccak256(abi.encode(T_NODE, bytes32 l, bytes32 r))

core must contain saltCommit, never the entitlement salt. Absent fiscalHash and delegationHash use the ABSENT sentinel (see §8 for delegationHash).

6.3 Salt commitments and entitlement IDs

saltCommit    = keccak256(abi.encode(T_SC, bytes32 entitlementSalt))
entitlementId = keccak256(abi.encode(T_ENT, bytes32 receiptRoot, uint256 lineIndex, bytes32 saltCommit))

Documents and proofs carry saltCommit only. Because deriving an entitlement ID requires saltCommit (which is public) but registering or claiming it requires the salt preimage (a bearer secret), a party holding a proof cannot register or claim the corresponding entitlement (see §14).

6.4 Signatures and EIP-712 digests

Receipt commitments and delegation certificates are signed with EIP-712 typed data ([EIP-712]). The domain is deliberately chain-agnostic (no chainId or verifyingContract), so a receipt verifies on any chain:

DOMAIN_TYPEHASH     = keccak256("EIP712Domain(string name,string version)")
domainSeparator     = keccak256(abi.encode(DOMAIN_TYPEHASH, keccak256("VRS"), keccak256("1")))

COMMITMENT_TYPEHASH = keccak256("ReceiptCommitment(bytes32 receiptRoot,bytes32 schemaHash)")
commitmentDigest    = keccak256(0x1901 ++ domainSeparator ++
                       keccak256(abi.encode(COMMITMENT_TYPEHASH, receiptRoot, schemaHash)))

DELEGATION_TYPEHASH = keccak256("DelegationCert(address operationalKey,bytes32 role,uint64 validFrom,uint64 validUntil)")
delegationDigest    = keccak256(0x1901 ++ domainSeparator ++
                       keccak256(abi.encode(DELEGATION_TYPEHASH, operationalKey, role, validFrom, validUntil)))

The issuer signs commitmentDigest(receiptRoot, schemaHash). Signatures must be 65-byte ECDSA (r,s,v) with v ∈ {27,28} and low-s (the upper bound is the secp256k1 half-order); verifiers must reject malleable or zero-recovery signatures. Holder-side signatures (§9.4, §14) must support EOA recovery and should support [ERC-1271] smart-account validation. Issuers whose keys are fiscalisation devices may instead sign with COSE/X.509 under a fiscal-evidence profile.

6.5 Merkle proof processing

A Merkle path is processed against a leaf and its 0-based index (leaf position) by combining with the tagged node hash, choosing order by the low bit of the index at each level:

processProof(leaf, index, path):
    cur = leaf
    for sibling in path:
        cur = (index & 1 == 0) ? node(cur, sibling) : node(sibling, cur)
        index = index >> 1
    return cur

The header leaf is processed at index = 0; line item i at index = i + 1. A VRS v1 receipt must not exceed a Merkle path length of 16 (§7.4); verifiers must reject any proof with a longer path. Anchor-batch trees use the structurally distinct T_ANODE tag so that receipt-tree and anchor-tree proofs cannot be confused.

7. Receipt document model Normative

7.1 Receipt types

receiptTypeMeaning
sale / 0Sale receipt (default).
delivery / 1Delivery receipt (issuer role courier, or a recipient countersignature).
service / 2Service receipt (issuer role repairer/installer).
credit / 3Credit/correction receipt (§12).

All four types share the signing scheme, Merkle structure, and attestation model; they differ only in receiptType and the issuer role attested for the issuer.

7.2 Header

The header leaf (§6.2) commits to: the signing issuer address; issuedAt; receiptType; fiscalHash (or ABSENT); and delegationHash (or ABSENT). The receipt document additionally carries the schemaHash (signed via the commitment digest) and the issuer signature. Issuer identity must resolve to a root identity under the verifier's accepted policy (§8).

7.3 Line-item groups and fields

GroupRequiredFields (type — unit)
corerequiredlineIndex (uint256), productId (bytes — UTF-8 GS1 element string, or gtin:/sku:/txt: prefixed), qtyMilli (uint256 — quantity ×1000), saltCommit (bytes32)
commercialoptionalunitPriceMinor (uint256 — minor units), taxRateBps (uint32 — basis points), currency (bytes3 — ISO 4217 alpha-3)
warrantyoptionalwarrantor (bytes — e.g. gln:…), durationDays (uint32 — days), termsHash (bytes32 — keccak256(JCS(terms)))
descriptiveoptionalcommitted value (bytes32 — keccak256(JCS(descriptive object)))
provenanceoptionalbatchId (bytes32), serialSaltCommit (bytes32) — manufacturer endorsement (§16)

Tax rate must be expressed in basis points (taxRateBps), not parts per million; warranty duration must be expressed in days. Absent optional groups must use the ABSENT sentinel as the group hash.

7.4 Commitment scheme

The receipt is a Merkle tree. The header leaf must be at leaf index 0; line item i at leaf index i + 1. The tree must be padded to a power of two using the PAD sentinel as filler leaves. The receipt root is the tree root and is the value the issuer signs (via the commitment digest). A v1 receipt must not exceed depth 16: header plus all line items must fit within 216 = 65 536 leaves (at most 65 535 line items). The commitment tags and field order are frozen for v1.

7.5 Commercial core and fiscal evidence

The commercial core (issuer, issue time, receipt type, committed line items, quantities, disclosed amounts, and entitlement-derivation material) must be independently verifiable without understanding any jurisdiction's tax regime. Tax, accounting, and invoice-compliance evidence must be modelled as fiscal evidence, bound via fiscalHash, headerExtensions["vrs.fiscal"], profile-defined references, or attachment hashes. If no fiscal evidence is present, fiscalHash must be ABSENT; if present, it must commit to a canonical fiscal-evidence descriptor (profile id, jurisdiction, external identifiers, document/payload hash, verification endpoint, latest known status, predecessor/successor links). Sensitive fiscal payload contents must not be written to the registry.

A verifier must distinguish: signature/root verification; commercial-core proof; fiscal-evidence binding; profile-specific fiscal validation; and tax-compliance assessment. A signature or proof must not be represented as proof of tax compliance unless a relevant fiscal profile has been validated and the verifier's policy accepts it. Example profile families: EU/Peppol/UBL (EN 16931), Japan qualified invoice, India GST e-invoice (IRN/QR), China digital fapiao, US expense evidence.

8. Issuer identity and delegation Normative

A VRS signature proves a key signed a commitment. It becomes an issuer signature only under a verifier-accepted issuer policy; VRS does not define a global identity system or require a DID. A verifier policy may accept any of: a local keyring; an issuer manifest (vrs/issuer-manifest/1.0) discovered at /.well-known/vrs/issuer.json; an anchored manifest hash/status in an accepted registry; a fiscal-evidence profile that identifies the issuer; a manufacturer/channel grant chain; or an attestation from a marketplace, PSP, GS1, regulator, or other accepted party.

Issuers should operate a three-tier key hierarchy: a cold root key (offline; establishes identity, signs major transitions, recovers from compromise; should not sign routine receipts); admin keys (publish manifests, rotate delegates, sign status); and live signing keys (short-lived, scoped, individually revocable delegates used by POS/ecommerce/service/manufacturing systems).

Delegation. When a delegate signs, the header's delegationHash must commit the delegation, and the root must sign a delegation certificate:

delegationHash = (rootIdentity == address(0))
   ? ABSENT
   : keccak256(abi.encode(T_DELEG, address rootIdentity, bytes32 role, uint64 validFrom, uint64 validUntil))

The certificate binds (operationalKey, role, validFrom, validUntil) and is signed by the root over delegationDigest (§6.4). A verifier must reject a delegated receipt whose certified role does not match the role it is verifying for: a key delegated for merchant must not be accepted as manufacturer or courier, even if the root is independently attested for that other role. A delegate signature must not be accepted outside its [validFrom, validUntil] window for the receipt's issuedAt. The effective issuer identity is the rootIdentity when delegated, else the signer; attestation checks and all issuer comparisons must use the effective identity.

Issuer status is dynamic (active, revoked, superseded, compromised, unknown). Verifiers should evaluate issuer/manifest/delegate status at both issuance time and verification time when available, and should quarantine receipts from a compromised key's affected window.

9. Proofs and presentations Normative

9.1 Disclosure levels

VRS supports selective disclosure. A proof declares its disclosure level — how much of the receipt it reveals — and the holder reveals only the sub-leaves that level requires, plus the Merkle path and signed root. The frozen identifiers are:

Disclosure levelIdentifierReveals
Full documentfullThe entire receipt.
CorecoreHeader plus a line's core group (no price).
Selected groupscore+<group>Header plus core plus the named groups (e.g. core+commercial, core+warranty).
Zero-knowledge (future)zkProves a statement about the receipt over the same commitments.

Disclosure level is a property of the proof; it is unrelated to Ethereum layer-1 / layer-2. The labels L0 / L1-core / L1-full / L2 used by earlier drafts are deprecated in favour of these identifiers.

A proof must disclose saltCommit, never the entitlement salt. Possession of a proof must not be treated as entitlement control once an entitlement is registered (§9.4).

9.2 Proof object

A line-item proof consists of a header disclosure and a core disclosure (field names follow the reference library):

HeaderDisclosure { address issuer; uint64 issuedAt; uint8 receiptType; bytes32 fiscalHash;
                   bytes32 schemaHash; bytes issuerSig; bytes32[] headerPath;
                   address rootIdentity;      // address(0) = undelegated
                   bytes32 delegRole; uint64 delegValidFrom; uint64 delegValidUntil; bytes rootSig; }

CoreDisclosure   { uint256 lineIndex; bytes productId; uint256 qtyMilli; bytes32 saltCommit;
                   bytes32 commercialHash; bytes32 warrantyHash; bytes32 descriptiveHash;
                   bytes32 provenanceHash; bytes32[] itemPath; }

An undisclosed group is represented by its group hash (or ABSENT); a disclosed group supplies the preimage fields from which the verifier recomputes the group hash.

9.3 Verification algorithm

To verify a core-disclosure line-item proof, a verifier must:

  1. Require headerPath.length == itemPath.length and ≤ 16; otherwise reject.
  2. Compute delegationHash from the delegation fields; compute the header leaf; compute receiptRoot = processProof(headerLeaf, 0, headerPath).
  3. Compute coreHash from the disclosed core fields; compute itemLeaf; require processProof(itemLeaf, lineIndex + 1, itemPath) == receiptRoot.
  4. Recover the signer from commitmentDigest(receiptRoot, schemaHash) and the issuer signature; require it equals issuer.
  5. If delegated (rootIdentity != 0): require issuedAt ∈ [delegValidFrom, delegValidUntil]; recover the root from delegationDigest(issuer, delegRole, delegValidFrom, delegValidUntil) and rootSig; require it equals rootIdentity; the effective identity is rootIdentity. Otherwise the effective identity is issuer.
  6. Apply issuer policy to the effective identity (§8); for role-scoped verifiers, require delegRole matches the verifier's role.
  7. Derive entitlementId = entitlementId(receiptRoot, lineIndex, saltCommit) and productIdHash = keccak256(productId) for downstream use.

The cost is one ECDSA recovery, ~log(n) keccaks for the path, and one attestation lookup. The verifier is stateless and permissionless; consuming logic adds its own policy (§18).

9.4 Holder challenge

A proof object is copyable and therefore confers no control over a registered entitlement. For a registered entitlement, a verifier that needs to know the presenter controls it must require a holder challenge signed by the current registered holder key or smart account. A challenge should carry an entitlementId, a fresh nonce, an audience, and an expiry, and must be verified against holderOf(entitlementId) using EOA recovery or [ERC-1271].

10. References and claim links Normative

VRS defines the URI schemes:

vrs1:ref:<https-url>          // powerless, shareable
vrs1:claim:<url-or-token-url>  // bearer authority; redeemed once
vrs1:proof:<encoded-proof>
vrs1:doc:<encoded-document>

vrs1:ref is powerless and shareable. vrs1:claim carries (or leads to) bearer authority and must not be requested by desks or verifiers where vrs1:ref or vrs1:proof suffices. GET /ref/{refId} must return a vrs/ref-response/1.0 object containing the powerless document (or reference) and the credit graph known to the origin channel, and must omit bearer secrets, claim tokens, register authorisations, and wallet keys; it should use private cache headers. Claim redemption must be one-time or otherwise payer-bound by an explicit profile, and the service must mark a claim token consumed before returning bearer secrets.

11. Bearer secrets and the holder archive Normative

Receipt documents and bearer secrets are separate artifacts. A bearer-secret payload (vrs/secrets/1.0) carries entitlement salts, serial salts, and other bearer authority, and must not appear in documents, ref responses, or ordinary proofs. .vrsbox is the portable holder archive; it may contain receipt documents, bearer secrets, registrations, lifecycle documents, and cached credit graphs/manifests/attestations, and must not contain holder private keys unless a separate wallet profile permits it. Holder agents must preserve the document/secret separation and must support .vrsbox export where they act as custodians.

12. Credit documents and credit graphs Normative

A credit receipt (receiptType: credit) must reference its origin via headerExtensions["vrs.credit"].originRoot. Per credited line it must identify the origin line index, credited quantity and/or amount, and a reason (return, price-adjustment, tax-correction, goodwill). Credit lines derive no entitlement (their saltCommit is a sentinel). Verifiers consuming receipts for expenses, tax, rewards, returns, or credits must deduplicate credit documents and must reject over-crediting per their policy domain.

Origin channels and refs should expose vrs/credit-graph/1.0. A credit graph must distinguish complete, partial, paginated, unavailable, account-gated, and retention-expired states, and must not silently omit old credits while claiming completeness. The graph service is a discovery mechanism and is not trusted for truth; verifiers must verify every credit document.

Receipts are immutable. Corrections, cancellations, and reissues must be represented as additional signed documents, registry state, or external fiscal-status records; an implementation must not replace an original receipt with a corrected one while preserving the same proof surface. Three ledgers remain distinct: VRS commercial documents; VRS entitlement registry status; and external fiscal-evidence status (active/cancelled/corrected/reissued/voided/unknown).

13. Canonical registry state Normative

For each ecosystem, VRS state is read from the canonical deployment identified by a signed deployment manifest. Noncanonical registry state must not be treated as VRS state. Verifiers, holder agents, and services must fetch or bundle signed deployment manifests, verify manifest hashes and signatures, select registry addresses from the accepted manifest, and warn on wrong, unknown, or noncanonical registries. The default state profile is the Ethereum State Profile (Appendix B); the document layer remains chain-neutral.

14. Entitlement registry Normative

A VRS entitlement registry must support: registration; holder, first-holder, status, and issuer lookup; transfer and transferWithAuth; stolen flag/clear; returned/voided state; quantity-accounted bearer returns; and lifecycle event-hash logging. State per entitlement is { status, holder, firstHolder, issuer } with status ∈ {Unregistered, Active, Returned, Voided, Stolen}.

Registration must be consumer-pull (the issuer never transacts) and must establish two facts independently:

  • Issuer binding. The recorded issuer must be recovered from the issuer's receipt-commitment signature (an IssuerBinding of {receiptRoot, schemaHash, issuerSig, +delegation}), never asserted by the caller.
  • Bearer authority. The entitlement id must be derived as entitlementId(receiptRoot, lineIndex, saltCommit) with saltCommit = keccak256(abi.encode(T_SC, salt)) and salt the revealed bearer secret. A party holding only a proof (which carries saltCommit, not salt) therefore cannot register the corresponding id.

Registration must not require the productId, quantity, or any line-item group to be disclosed on chain: only the opaque receiptRoot and the issuer identity are written. Because the bearer secret is revealed in the registration call, deployments on a public mempool must offer a commit–reveal variant binding the reveal to the registrant (commitment = keccak256(abi.encode(salt, registrant)) with a minimum commit age). firstHolder must be set once and must not change through transfer, recovery, resale, or gift. transferWithAuth lets a third party submit a holder-signed authorisation binding (entitlementId, to, nonce, deadline). Stolen, returned, and voided values are registry state, not legal determinations. See Appendix B for the contract interface.

15. Lifecycle attestations Normative

Lifecycle attestations (vrs/lifecycle-attestation/1.0) are signed off-chain records bound to an entitlement, consisting of a shared envelope, a typed event body (service, repair, inspection, owner note, appraisal, recall action, …), a signature, optional attachment hashes, and optional privacy metadata. The registry may record recordEvent(entitlementId, attestationHash) where attestationHash = keccak256(JCS(attestation)). The chain must not store lifecycle contents, locations, invoices, photos, addresses, or service notes. Holder agents must support selective lifecycle disclosure; entitlement transfer does not automatically transfer lifecycle document access. Provenance-grade events should carry proven time (anchoring): a record anchored at block B demonstrably existed by B, defeating backdating. The reference verifier marks any history entry that has only asserted time.

16. Manufacturing and serial authentication Normative

A manufacturer-endorsed line carries a provenance group binding batchId and serialSaltCommit (= keccak256(abi.encode(T_SC, serialSalt))); the group is ABSENT for unendorsed lines. The manufacturer profile defines batch creation, a serial root (membership tree under T_SNODE), mint-allowance/distribution regimes, a serial nullifier (keccak256(T_NULL, batchId, serial, …)), and channel assurance via public allowance traces or off-chain grant chains (vrs/grant-chain/1.0). Authentication reveals the serial salt and sets the nullifier exactly once; a second use of the same serial reverts, which is the cloned-serial signal. On a public mempool a commit–reveal variant should be used.

VRS serial authentication proves serial/receipt/channel evidence and detects cloned serial use. It does not prove physical genuineness. A user interface must not label an item "genuine" solely because VRS evidence verifies. Provenance for resale is a three-part check: a Digital Product Passport says what the item is; the entitlement says who holds the rights; the receipt says where and when it was sold.

17. Recall notices Normative profile (draft)

A recall notice (vrs/recall-notice/1.0) is a signed document keyed on a manufacturing batchId (with optional finer scope), carrying severity, action (stop-use/return/repair/update), a remedyHash (full text off-chain), and issuedAt. It names no owner and carries no contact detail. The notice hash should be anchored under its batch in a notice registry (an event Recalled(batchId, noticeHash, issuerRoot, severity) with batchId indexed), reusing proven-time anchoring. A recall should be honoured only if its issuer is accepted for the batch under the verifier's policy (manufacturer root / grant chain, or a regulator attested for a recall role).

Holder agents should match recalls locally: because each endorsed line binds batchId in its provenance group, the wallet knows the owner's exposure and watches Recalled(batchId) for those batches, fetching the remedy by hash/URI. The manufacturer broadcasts once per batch and learns nothing about who holds the batch; the match reaches the current holder regardless of how many times the item changed hands. VRS supplies the authenticated notice and the broadcast-by-batch primitive; it does not mandate a transport, run a relay, or hold contact details. The anchored notice constitutes constructive notice; opt-in push channels add actual notice. The same mechanism generalises to firmware updates, warranty extensions, and end-of-life notices.

18. On-chain consumption Normative

A stateless verifier contract may expose verifyLineItem(HeaderDisclosure, CoreDisclosure, …) returning (receiptRoot, entitlementId, productIdHash, identity) after performing the checks of §9.3 against its configured attester policy and role. A consuming contract layers its own policy on top. Contracts that grant a once-per-item benefit (a reward, a token-gate, an escrow release) must bind consumption to the entitlementId (e.g. a claimed[entId] flag), never to the presenter, so that an item cannot farm the benefit repeatedly as it changes hands. Where current ownership matters, the contract must additionally require a holder challenge (§9.4) or read holderOf. Disclosure to a public chain is permanent; a contract should not require the commercial group unless the price is genuinely needed.

19. Service and agent requirements Normative

Claim/ref service. A conforming service must serve powerless refs; redeem claim tokens one-time or payer-bound; never serve bearer secrets from ref endpoints; expose known credit graphs beside origin receipts; support .vrsbox export where it stores holder data; support ref rotation where refs are shareable capability URLs; and document retention, deletion, recovery, logging, and payer-binding behaviour.

Holder agent. A conforming holder agent must preserve document/secret separation; import and export .vrsbox; use canonical deployment manifests before registry writes; support at least one registration path; support holder-challenge signing for registered items; support direct transfer or transferWithAuth; never include bearer secrets in ordinary presentations; support selective lifecycle disclosure; document custody mode, key-recovery limits, and privacy defaults; and distinguish registered holder from legal owner in user-facing contexts. Key recovery is not part of the VRS core; consumer pilots involving registered valuables should prefer ERC-1271 smart accounts with explicit, delayed, cancellable recovery, and must preserve firstHolder and allow .vrsbox migration.

20. Privacy and security considerations Normative + Informative

Implementations must: keep bearer secrets out of documents, refs, and ordinary proofs; register entitlements without disclosing productId, quantity, or groups on chain; distinguish key-signature verification from issuer identity resolution; avoid writing sensitive lifecycle contents on chain; distinguish cached/indexed state from verified canonical state; warn on noncanonical registries; support selective disclosure where holder privacy is at risk; and avoid claiming legal title, genuineness, possession, or complete history without external evidence. Implementations should: use fresh holder addresses per entitlement where practical; support ref rotation; minimise ref-endpoint logging; use commit–reveal on public mempools; and prefer smart-account recovery for consumer valuables.

Informative — ownership privacy direction The Ethereum State Profile today stores holder/firstHolder as plaintext addresses and emits transfer events, so a passive observer can reconstruct an item's ownership chain. The planned direction replaces the plaintext mapping with a commitment accumulator plus a nullifier set and routes holders to stealth addresses (with gas paid externally), so that an observer learns only that some entitlement had an event. Residual risks include the linkability of a unique serialised item across presentations, and the non-post-quantum nature of the current primitives. See the design notes in Reference/03-extensions-and-analysis/.

Informative — privacy and power Verifiable ownership and purchase records are arriving by mandate (digital identity wallets, age verification, e-invoicing, the EU Digital Product Passport). A standard built carelessly would be a surveillance tool. VRS aims to make that infrastructure default to the user's control: the consumer holds the data, discloses selectively, puts nothing on a public ledger, keeps no central registry of who owns what, and pays no fee.

21. Forward compatibility Informative

VRS freezes only the v1 commitment surface and pushes everything else to profiles and version boundaries. Every domain tag is suffixed :v1, so a future suite gets its own namespace; the signed schemaHash gives each receipt an explicit version anchor. The five-group leaf supports predicate disclosure without re-opening unrelated groups; the bearer salt is shaped to double as a zero-knowledge nullifier; issuer identity lives in a manifest that declares its keys (so a new, including post-quantum, signature suite can arrive per issuer). The one genuine tension is that keccak256 is convenient on-chain but expensive inside a SNARK; whether ZK predicates must work over existing v1 receipts (STARK-class proving or recursion) or only over a future commitment suite is the decision a v2 would make. Reserved future levers include a SNARK-friendly mirror commitment, an accumulator-shaped attester set, per-circuit domain tags, and cross-receipt aggregation.
